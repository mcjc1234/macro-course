Day 1 — Core Concepts

Eight interconnected ideas that underpin the whole course. Work through them in order for the clearest picture.

What is macroeconomics?
Macroeconomics looks at the economy as a whole — not individual markets, but the total picture. Think of it like a jigsaw puzzle: microeconomics studies each piece independently; macroeconomics studies the whole image.
Microeconomics
Individual markets, firms, consumers — price of one good
Macroeconomics
Total output, national income, price level, employment, trade
The three manager questions
Question 1
What drives economic activity?
Question 2
How can policy influence it?
Question 3
What are the implications for business?
Measuring economic activity — GDP
GDP is the value of all output produced by resources in the domestic economy. Three methods measure it — and all three should give the same answer.
Output method
Sum of all goods & services produced (Y)
Income method
Sum of wages, profits, rent, dividends
Expenditure method
Sum of all spending in the economy
Y = C + I + G + (X − M)

GDP vs GNP: GDP = output within borders. GNP = GDP + net income earned by residents abroad.

Real vs Nominal: Always strip out inflation. Real GDP growth is what actually matters — higher prices don't mean more output.
The circular flow of income
The economy runs in a loop. Households supply labour to firms; firms pay wages; households spend on goods; the cycle continues. Five sectors make up the full model.
Household sector
Firm sector
Financial sector
Government sector
Foreign sector
= Real GDP
Leakages (drain the loop)
Savings · Taxes · Imports
Injections (boost the loop)
Investment · Govt spending · Exports
Equilibrium: Leakages = Injections
When injections exceed leakages, the economy grows. When leakages exceed injections, it contracts. This is the engine behind the multiplier effect.
Aggregate Demand & Aggregate Supply
The AD/AS model is the workhorse of macroeconomics. It shows how the price level and real GDP are determined — and what happens when they're out of balance.
Aggregate Demand (AD)
Total spending at each price level. Downward sloping — higher prices reduce real purchasing power.
Aggregate Supply (AS)
Total output firms produce. Upward sloping short-run; vertical at full employment long-run.
AD = C + I + G + (X − M)
Inflationary gap
AD exceeds full employment output → prices rise
Deflationary gap
AD falls short of full employment → unemployment rises
Equilibrium is where AD = AS. Policy aims to keep the economy at that point — which is why fiscal and monetary tools exist.
Macroeconomic policy objectives — the "Magic Hexagon"
Governments pursue several goals simultaneously — often in tension with each other.
High, sustained economic growth
Full employment of resources
Low & stable inflation
Sustainable balance of payments
Stable exchange rate
Within environmental limits
Policy target
Quantifiable aim — e.g. 2% inflation
Policy instrument
The lever used — e.g. interest rates
Policy goal
Deeper purpose — e.g. long-term full employment
Fiscal policy
Fiscal policy is the use of government spending (G) and taxation (T) to influence aggregate demand. It is the core Keynesian tool for managing the economy.
Expansionary
↑ G or ↓ Tax → shifts AD right → closes deflationary gap
Contractionary
↓ G or ↑ Tax → shifts AD left → closes inflationary gap
Multiplier = 1 ÷ (savings rate + tax rate + import rate)
The multiplier: An initial injection ripples through the economy. A £1bn boost in government spending can raise GDP by a multiple of that amount, depending on how much of each pound is re-spent rather than saved, taxed, or spent on imports.

Watch out for crowding out — when government borrowing pushes up interest rates and squeezes out private investment.
Money, banking & interest rates
Keynes identified three reasons people hold money rather than investing it:
Transactions motive
Need cash for day-to-day purchases
Precautionary motive
Buffer for unexpected needs ("rainy day")
Speculative motive
Hold cash to exploit investment opportunities — sensitive to interest rates
The interest rate is the price of money. Higher rates reduce investment and consumer spending, pulling down AD:
↑ Interest rate
→
↓ Investment

↓ AD

↓ Inflation / output
Schools of macroeconomic thought
Macroeconomic policy has evolved through competing schools of thought, each born from real-world crises.
Pre-Keynesian / Neo-Classical (pre-1930s)
Markets self-correct. Minimal government. Trust competitive markets for full employment.
Keynesian (1936–1970s)
Demand management via fiscal policy. Government fills gaps left by private sector.
Monetarist (1970s–80s)
Milton Friedman: control the money supply. Inflation is always a monetary phenomenon.
Supply-side (1980s–present)
Grow the economy's productive capacity: deregulation, privatisation, tax incentives.
The 2008 financial crisis and COVID-19 reignited Keynesian thinking — and the debate continues today.

Aggregate Planned Expenditure & Equilibrium

The economy reaches equilibrium when what businesses produce (Y) exactly equals what everyone plans to spend (AE). Work through the six steps to see how it works.

Imagine a small economy: "Coffeeton"
Coffeeton has one product: coffee. Workers earn wages (national income = Y), and those wages get spent on coffee. The government buys coffee for meetings, firms invest in new coffee machines, some coffee is exported, and some income leaks out when residents buy imported biscuits.
Aggregate Planned Expenditure (AE) is everything everyone intends to spend on domestically produced goods:

C and M change when income changes — they're endogenous. Investment, G, and X are set independently of income — firms and governments plan these in advance (exogenous). This is why the AE line has a slope less than 1.

Equilibrium: Y = AE
Equilibrium is where national income exactly equals planned spending. Coffeeton produces £100bn and people plan to spend exactly £100bn. No unsold stock, no empty shelves. There is no pressure to change.

Y = AE → Equilibrium. The economy is stable at Ye.
The 45° line explained simply
It's just all the points where Y = AE drawn on a graph. Draw a square — the diagonal is the 45° line. The AE schedule crosses it at exactly one point: equilibrium (Ye).
Important: equilibrium doesn't mean the economy is doing well — it just means it's stable. You can have a stable equilibrium with high unemployment. That's exactly where policy comes in.
Deflationary gap — equilibrium is too low
Coffeeton settles at equilibrium Y = £80bn — but its factories could produce £100bn at full employment. £20bn of capacity is idle. Workers are unemployed. The economy is stable, but stuck below its potential.

Real-world example: The UK furlough scheme during COVID-19 was designed to prevent the deflationary gap deepening — by keeping household income (C) from collapsing.
Inflationary gap — spending exceeds capacity
Confidence is high, everyone is spending freely. Planned spending pushes the implied equilibrium to £120bn — but Coffeeton's factories can only produce £100bn. The economy can't physically produce more. So what happens? Prices rise instead.

Real-world example: The post-pandemic spending surge (2021–22) created exactly this — pent-up demand smashing into limited supply. Central banks raised interest rates sharply to pull AE back down.
