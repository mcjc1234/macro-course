Session 1
Introduction to Macroeconomics
What is macroeconomics, why does it matter for managers, and what are governments actually trying to achieve?
Micro vs Macro — the jigsaw analogy
Microeconomics studies individual pieces of the jigsaw. Macroeconomics studies the complete picture.
Microeconomics
Why did the price of eggs rise 20% this year?
Macroeconomics
Why did the whole UK economy grow by 2%?
What macro actually studies
Think of the UK as a single entity. Macro asks: How much does it earn? How much does it spend? Are prices rising? How many people have jobs?
Total output
GDP / GNP
Price level
Inflation
Jobs
Employment
Trade
Balance of payments
A pub chain manager cares about micro (beer prices). The Chancellor cares about macro (whether 30 million people can afford to buy anything at all).
Why macro matters to you as a manager
The macro environment sets the water level your business swims in. Even a perfectly run company drowns in a deep recession.
Interest rates rise 3%
Your mortgage costs surge. Consumer spending drops. Your sales fall.
Inflation at 10%
Your input costs soar. Wage demands rise. Margins squeezed.
GDP grows 4%
Consumer confidence high. Hiring easy. Investment pays off.
The three macro questions for managers: (1) What's driving activity? (2) What will policy do next? (3) What does it mean for my business?
The "Magic Hexagon" — what governments are trying to do
No government can achieve all six simultaneously. They constantly trade off against each other.
High sustained growth
Full employment
Low & stable inflation
Sustainable trade balance
Stable exchange rate
Environmental limits
The classic trade-off: inflation vs unemployment
Push unemployment down too far (boost spending) and inflation rises. Crush inflation (cut spending, raise rates) and unemployment rises. Governments are always navigating between these two.
Example: 2022 UK — Bank of England raised rates 14 times to fight 11% inflation, knowing it would slow the economy and cost jobs.
Four schools of thought — the intellectual map
Every policy debate you'll see in the news traces back to one of these four traditions.
Pre-Keynesian (pre-1930s)
Markets self-correct. Government: stay out. Trust the invisible hand.
Keynesian (1936–70s)
Markets fail. Government must spend to fill the gap. Furlough scheme = modern Keynesianism.
Monetarist (1970s–80s)
Inflation is the enemy. Control the money supply. Thatcher's Britain.
Supply-side (1980s–now)
Grow productive capacity. Deregulate. Cut taxes on business. Laffer curve.
The 2008 crash and COVID both caused temporary Keynesian revivals — even monetarist governments ended up spending massively. The debate never really ends.
Session 2
Measuring Economic Activity
GDP, GNP, real vs nominal, and why the numbers in the news can be misleading.
GDP — the economy's scorecard
GDP (Gross Domestic Product) = the total value of everything produced within a country's borders in a year. Think of it as the economy's annual revenue figure.
Y = C + I + G + (X − M)
C — Consumers
£1,400bn
I — Investment
£400bn
G — Government
£500bn
X−M — Net exports
−£100bn
Illustrative UK GDP ≈ £2,200bn. Consumer spending is always the largest component — roughly 60%.
GDP
Output by resources within UK borders (includes foreign-owned factories in the UK)
GNP
Income earned by UK citizens (includes Brits working abroad, excludes foreigners here)
Three ways to measure — same answer, different angles
Imagine a bakery makes 100 loaves. You can measure the economy three ways — and all should give the same total.
Output method
Count every loaf baked across the whole economy. Sum of value added.
Income method
Add up baker wages + flour supplier profits + landlord rent. All income = all output.
Expenditure method
Add up everything spent on the loaves: households, firms, government, net exports.
In reality they rarely match perfectly — ONS averages them to get the "best estimate". The gaps reveal data quality problems (e.g. cash-in-hand economy isn't captured).
Why "value added" matters in the output method
A wheat farmer sells grain to a miller for £10. The miller sells flour to a baker for £20. The baker sells bread for £30. GDP = £30 (not £60) — we only count the final value to avoid double counting.
Real vs Nominal — the inflation trap
If GDP rises from £2,000bn to £2,200bn, is the economy really 10% richer? Not if prices rose 10% — you're just paying more for the same stuff.
Nominal GDP (Year 1)
£2,000bn at Year 1 prices
Nominal GDP (Year 2)
£2,200bn at Year 2 prices
Real GDP (Year 2)
£2,050bn — after stripping out 7% inflation
Real GDP growth = 2.5%. Not 10%. The GDP deflator removed inflation from the comparison.
This is why all official growth statistics are "real" figures. When politicians quote GDP growth, inflation has already been removed. Always ask: nominal or real?
Practical implication
Your company revenue grew 8% last year. Inflation was 6%. Your real sales growth was only 2%. Macro thinking helps you understand your true performance.
Why GDP isn't a measure of wellbeing
GDP goes up when you get divorced (legal fees), when there's a car crash (repairs, hospital), and when people work longer hours. It misses everything unpaid or illegal.
GDP ignores
Volunteering, childcare, inequality, environmental damage, happiness, leisure time
GDP includes
War spending, cigarette sales, gambling — all raise GDP
Example: Norway and Qatar both have high GDP per capita. But Norway consistently ranks top for wellbeing; Qatar has very high inequality and poor labour rights. Same number, very different reality.
Leading, lagging and coincident indicators
Economists watch other signals too. House-building starts (leading — warns of changes 12+ months ahead). Car sales (leading — months ahead). Retail sales (coincident — tells you now). Unemployment (lagging — tells you after it happened).

Equilibrium: leakages = injections
The economy is in equilibrium (stable, no tendency to change) when the total money draining out equals the total money flowing in.
S + T + M = I + G + X

This is why export booms stimulate growth and why tax rises can trigger recessions. Both change the leakage/injection balance.
The multiplier — a boulder in a pond
An initial injection doesn't just raise income by its own amount. It ripples through the economy, each round of spending generating more income.
Example: Government builds a £1bn hospital
Construction workers earn £1bn. They spend 80% (save 20%). That £800m goes to retailers, who spend 80% of that (£640m)... and so on. Each round is smaller because of the savings leakage.

£800m

£640m

£5,000m !
Multiplier = 1 ÷ MPS = 1 ÷ 0.20 = 5
With only savings as a leakage (MPS=20%), a £1bn injection generates £5bn of total national income. In reality there are three leakages (tax and imports too), making the real multiplier much smaller — typically 1.2 to 2.

Determination of Economic Activity
Aggregate planned expenditure, the 45° diagram, and inflationary/deflationary gaps — explained through a coffee town.
Aggregate Planned Expenditure — what everyone intends to spend
AE is the total spending all sectors plan to make on domestically produced goods. It's not what was actually spent — it's the intention.
AE = C + I + G + (X − M)

Session 5
Aggregate Demand & Aggregate Supply
The AD/AS model — how price levels and output interact, and what happens when things are out of balance.
Aggregate Demand — why it slopes downward
AD = total spending on domestically produced goods at each price level. When prices rise, people buy less. Two mechanisms drive this.
AD = C + I + G + (X − M)
Real money balance effect
Prices rise → your £1,000 savings buy less → you feel poorer → you spend less
Substitution effect
Prices rise → interest rates rise → borrowing is expensive → investment and consumer durables fall; imports look cheap → exports fall
What shifts the entire AD curve?
When one of the components (C, I, G, X−M) changes independently of the price level, the whole curve shifts. Rightward = more demand at every price. Leftward = less.
AD shifts right (expansion)
Tax cut → C rises. Government builds HS2 → G rises. China boom → X rises.
AD shifts left (contraction)
Rate hike → I falls. Austerity → G falls. Recession abroad → X falls.
Aggregate Supply — two very different time horizons
AS = total output firms produce at each price level. The short-run and long-run look completely different.
Short-run AS (upward sloping)
Higher prices → firms produce more (higher profit margins). Workers haven't renegotiated wages yet.
Long-run AS (vertical)
At full employment, the economy can't produce more regardless of price. Only real capacity improvements shift this.
The short-run AS slope depends on labour market flexibility. If workers immediately demand higher wages when prices rise, AS is nearly vertical. If wages are sticky, AS is flatter and firms can expand output temporarily.
What shifts AS?
Technology improvements, better infrastructure, education, immigration, energy price changes, business regulation. A new oil discovery shifts LRAS right. A war destroying factories shifts it left.
Macroeconomic equilibrium: AD = AS
The economy settles where AD meets AS. This gives us a specific price level (P*) and real GDP (Y*). If prices are above P*, unsold goods pile up and firms cut prices. Below P*, queues form and prices rise. The market finds P* naturally.
At equilibrium (e)
P* = 100, Y* = £2,000bn. No surplus, no shortage.
If P > P*
Firms over-produce, goods pile up, prices fall back to P*
If P < P*
Queues form, firms raise prices, P rises back to P*
The three equilibrium states: Deflationary gap (Y* < Yfe) · Full employment (Y* = Yfe) · Inflationary gap (Y* > Yfe implied)
What happens when AD shifts right?
Government increases spending by £50bn. The AD curve shifts right. The new equilibrium has higher output AND higher prices. How much of each depends on the AS curve's shape.
Starting point
P = 100, Y = £1,800bn (below full employment)
After AD shifts right
P = 104, Y = £1,950bn. More output, slight inflation.
At full capacity
Further AD shifts only cause inflation, not more output
This explains why stimulus works during recessions (slack in the economy absorbs more output) but causes inflation during booms (economy is already at capacity). Timing is everything.
COVID-19 through the AD/AS lens
March 2020: AS crashed (lockdown — factories closed) AND AD crashed (no one could spend). Initial effect: falling output, falling prices. 2021: AD bounced back via furlough. AS recovered slowly. Result: inflationary gap. Central banks raised rates to push AD left.
Stagflation — the nightmare scenario
Normally inflation and unemployment move in opposite directions. Stagflation breaks this rule: high inflation AND high unemployment together. It happened in the 1970s oil crisis.
How stagflation happens: AS shifts left
1973: OPEC quadrupled oil prices overnight. Oil is an input for almost everything. Production costs soared for every firm. The SRAS curve shifted left — same prices but less output. Result: prices rose AND output fell AND unemployment rose. Traditional demand management couldn't fix it (boosting demand just adds more inflation).
Oil shock
Costs surge → SRAS shifts left
Output falls
GDP ↓, unemployment ↑
Prices rise
Inflation ↑ simultaneously
Stagflation: the worst of both worlds. Supply-side solutions (deregulation, competition) become necessary.
Session 6
Managing the Economy — An Overview
Targets, instruments and goals — the full toolkit governments use to steer the economy, and the trade-offs they face.
Three levels of policy intent
Governments operate at three levels simultaneously. Confusing them leads to bad analysis and bad headlines.
Policy Target
Specific, measurable number. "Inflation below 2%." "Unemployment below 4%."
Policy Instrument
The actual lever pulled. Interest rates. Tax rates. Government spending levels.
Policy Goal
The deeper purpose. Full employment. Price stability. Sustainable growth. Long-run aspiration.
Example: Bank of England 2022
Goal: long-run price stability. Target: CPI inflation back to 2%. Instrument: raised Bank Rate from 0.1% to 5.25% across 14 consecutive meetings. The instrument (rate) is not the goal (stability) — it's just the lever.
Politicians often confuse targets with goals. "Getting debt down" is a target, not a goal. The goal is sustainable growth and full employment. You might need to run deficits to achieve that goal.
The full policy toolkit
Governments have four main demand-side levers and one supply-side lever.
Fiscal policy
Change G or T to shift AD directly. Keynes' favourite. Fast impact on demand, slow on supply.
Monetary policy
Change interest rates or money supply to influence C and I. Central bank's main tool.
Exchange rate policy
Intervene in currency markets to affect competitiveness of exports and price of imports.
Trade policy
Tariffs, quotas, subsidies to influence X and M directly. Brexit changed the UK's trade policy fundamentally.
Supply-side policy
Deregulation, privatisation, education, R&D investment. Shifts LRAS right. Slow but lasting.
The unavoidable trade-offs
Every policy choice involves a trade-off. There is no free lunch in macroeconomics.
Inflation vs Unemployment
Cut rates to boost jobs → inflation rises. Hike rates to crush inflation → unemployment rises. You can't eliminate both simultaneously.
Growth vs Environment
Faster growth usually means more emissions, more resource use. The "green transition" is expensive short-term.
Trade balance vs Growth
Growing fast sucks in imports. A trade deficit worsens. Fixing the deficit requires slower growth or a currency devaluation.
Equality vs Efficiency
Redistribution via taxes reduces incentives. But extreme inequality reduces consumption and social stability.
The "Magic Hexagon" exists because ALL six objectives cannot be fully achieved at once. Policy is always about choosing which trade-off to accept this year.
The barrel model — visualising capacity
Think of the economy as a barrel. The barrel's size = productive capacity (LRAS). The water level = actual output (real GDP). The taps = injections. The holes = leakages.
Full barrel
Economy at full capacity. Any more water (demand) just overflows as inflation.
Half full
Economy below capacity. Unemployment. Deflationary gap. Open the taps (fiscal/monetary stimulus).
Growing barrel
Supply-side policies grow the barrel. More capacity = higher potential output without inflation.
Demand-side policy fills the barrel. Supply-side policy makes the barrel bigger. Both matter — but work on different timescales.
UK productivity puzzle: the barrel hasn't grown much since 2008. Supply-side reforms (infrastructure, skills, R&D) are needed to grow potential output — not just filling the barrel with more demand.
Session 7
Fiscal Policy & Government Finances
How governments use spending and taxation to manage the economy — the multiplier, crowding out, and the limits of fiscal policy.
The two fiscal levers: G and T
Fiscal policy is simple in theory: if the economy is too cold, spend more or tax less. If it's too hot, spend less or tax more. In practice, the politics are brutal.
Government spending (G)
NHS pay rises, road building, furlough, Universal Credit. Direct injection into AD.
Taxation (T)
Income tax, VAT, corporation tax, National Insurance. Changes household disposable income → affects C.
Direct vs indirect taxes
Direct taxes (income tax, corporation tax) affect the MPC — steeper AE slope. Indirect taxes (VAT) affect prices directly. The mechanics differ but both pull on the same lever: aggregate demand.
Budget balance = Tax revenue (T) − Government spending (G)
Surplus (T > G)
Government takes more out than it puts in. Contractionary.
Balanced
T = G. Neutral stance.
Deficit (G > T)
Government puts more in than it takes out. Expansionary. Adds to national debt.
Closing a deflationary gap: Keynesian stimulus
Economy stuck at £1,600bn, full employment potential is £2,000bn. Gap = £400bn. But thanks to the multiplier, government doesn't need to spend £400bn to close it.
Deflationary gap
£400bn — economy running below potential
Multiplier = 2
1 ÷ (MPS 0.2 + MRT 0.2 + MPM 0.1) = 2
Government only needs to spend
£200bn (multiplier does the rest)
£200bn extra G × multiplier of 2 = £400bn extra GDP. Gap closed.
Closing an inflationary gap: contractionary policy
Economy overheating — inflation rising. Government raises taxes by £100bn (reducing household disposable income → C falls). Multiplier works in reverse. AD shifts left. Inflation cools. But growth slows too.
↑ Tax £100bn × multiplier 2 = −£200bn fall in GDP. Inflationary gap reduced but growth sacrificed.
Crowding out — the hidden cost of borrowing
When the government borrows to fund a deficit, it competes with private firms for funds in financial markets. This pushes interest rates up — and private investment drops.

→
↑ Borrowing demand
→
↑ Interest rates
→
↓ Private investment I
→

The monetarist critique: If crowding out is complete, government spending just replaces private spending pound for pound. Net GDP effect = zero. Keynesians argue crowding out is only partial. The debate continues.
Why fiscal policy is harder than it looks
Four real limitations mean fiscal fine-tuning often misses:
Time lags
Diagnosing the problem: 6 months. Parliament approving spending: 6–12 months. Multiplier feeding through: 12–18 months. Total: 2+ years. Economy may have changed by then.
Uncertainty
Nobody knows the true multiplier. Nobody knows how close to full employment we are. Models disagree.
Budget deficit concerns
Running deficits year after year builds national debt. Markets eventually worry about repayment. Interest rates rise independently.
Structural unemployment
Fiscal stimulus can't retrain a steel worker to be a software engineer. Some unemployment doesn't respond to demand boosts.
This is why even Keynesian economists support "automatic stabilisers" (unemployment benefits rise automatically in recessions; taxes fall automatically) — they kick in without political lag.

Session 8
Money, Banking & Interest Rates
What money actually is, why people hold it, how banks create it, and how interest rates transmit through the economy.
Money — more than just notes and coins
Money is anything widely accepted as payment. It has three functions.
Medium of exchange
Avoids the barter problem. You don't need to find a baker who wants your shoes.
Store of value
Hold it now, spend later. (Inflation erodes this function.)
Unit of account
Price everything in the same unit. Allows rational comparison.
The money supply hierarchy
M0 (narrow money) = physical cash. M4 (broad money) = cash + bank deposits + near-money. Most money in the economy is not physical — it's entries on bank databases.
Physical cash in UK
~£80bn (M0)
Total money supply
~£2,800bn (M4)
Ratio
97% of money is bank deposits, not physical cash
Keynes's three motives for holding money (liquidity preference)
Why do people hold cash rather than investing it in interest-bearing assets? Keynes identified three separate reasons.
Transactions motive
You keep £500 in your current account to pay for groceries, fuel, coffee this week. Independent of interest rates.
Precautionary motive
You keep £2,000 as an emergency fund. "Rainy day" money. Also independent of interest rates.
Speculative motive
You hold £10,000 in cash waiting for bond prices to fall before buying. Very sensitive to interest rates.
The liquidity trap: When interest rates fall very low (near zero), the speculative motive dominates. Everyone holds cash expecting rates to rise (and bond prices to fall). No one invests. Monetary policy loses its grip. Japan 1990s, UK/US post-2008.
At near-zero rates: people hold cash, not bonds. Extra money supply sits idle. This is why quantitative easing had limited effect on real investment after 2008.
How banks create money — the credit multiplier
Banks don't just store money — they create it. When you deposit £1,000, the bank keeps a fraction (reserve ratio) and lends the rest.
Example: 10% reserve ratio, £1,000 initial deposit
You deposit £1,000. Bank keeps £100 (10%), lends £900 to someone else. That person deposits £900 in another bank, which keeps £90, lends £810. The £810 gets deposited... and so on.
Round 1
£1,000 deposit → £900 lent
Round 2
£900 deposit → £810 lent
Round 3
£810 → £729 lent
Total created
£10,000 (1/reserve ratio)
Money multiplier = 1 ÷ reserve ratio = 1 ÷ 0.10 = 10×
The 2008 crisis happened partly because reserve ratios fell dangerously low and banks created far too much credit money — then the music stopped.

Session 9
Monetary Policy
How central banks use interest rates and money supply to control inflation and support growth — and where it fails.
How does a rate change actually reach the real economy?
The Bank of England changes one number — Bank Rate. But money must travel through a whole system before it affects your job or your company's investment plans.
The five transmission channels
Each channel works at a different speed and with different strength.
1. Bank lending channel
BoE rate → commercial bank rates → mortgage and loan costs change → C and I adjust. Speed: weeks to months.
2. Asset price channel
Lower rates → bond prices rise → shares rise → people feel wealthier → they spend more (wealth effect). Speed: fast for markets, slower for spending.
3. Exchange rate channel
Higher UK rates → foreign investors buy £ → £ appreciates → exports more expensive → X falls, M rises → AD falls.
4. Expectations channel
If BoE signals rates will stay high, firms and households change behaviour immediately — even before rates move. The most powerful and most unpredictable.
Total transmission lag: 18–24 months. This is why central banks must act on future inflation, not current inflation. By the time today's rate rise bites, inflation may have already changed.
Quantity theory of money — the monetarist foundation
Milton Friedman's insight: "inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon." Too much money chasing too few goods.
MV = PY (Fisher Equation)
M
Money supply
V
Velocity (how often £1 is spent per year)
P
Price level
Y
Real output (GDP)
Example: the monetarist argument
Assume V and Y are roughly constant short-run. Economy: M = £1,000bn, V = 2, so PY = £2,000bn. Government prints money: M rises to £1,200bn. PY must rise to £2,400bn. If Y can't rise (full employment), P rises by 20%. Result: 20% inflation. Control M, control inflation.
Monetarist prescription: central bank controls money supply growth. Keep M growing at the same rate as real GDP, and inflation stays at zero. Simple in theory — hard in practice because V is unstable.
Quantitative Easing — monetary policy's last resort
When Bank Rate hits zero, the conventional lever is gone. QE is the alternative: the central bank creates new money and buys assets (government bonds) from financial institutions.
BoE creates £100bn
→
Buys government bonds from banks
→
Banks have £100bn cash
→
Bond prices ↑, yields ↓
→
All interest rates fall
→
AD stimulated
UK QE (2009–2021)
BoE created ~£895bn through QE. Prevented a deeper depression post-2008 and post-COVID.
The problem
Extra cash didn't always flow to businesses. Banks held it as reserves. Asset price inflation (house prices, shares) benefited the wealthy disproportionately.
Unwinding QE
2022–23: BoE started selling bonds back (Quantitative Tightening). Process is slow and complex.
QE inflated asset prices more than consumer prices — explaining much of the wealth inequality growth since 2008. A £1m house isn't more useful, it's just worth more money because the money supply expanded.
Where monetary policy fails
Monetary policy is powerful but has real blind spots. Understanding the limits explains why fiscal policy remains necessary.
Liquidity trap
At near-zero rates, cutting further does nothing. People and banks hoard cash. QE effectiveness falls sharply. Japan: trapped since 1995.
Supply-side inflation
Rate hikes can't fix an oil shock or a supply chain crisis. Raising rates to kill cost-push inflation also kills output. Stagflation trap.
Long lags
18–24 months for full effect. Risk of over-tightening: raising rates until they bite, then the economy collapses just as rates finally work.
Distribution effects
Rate rises hurt mortgage holders hardest. Rate cuts help asset owners most. Monetary policy is not distributionally neutral.
Keynesian vs Monetarist: the key disagreement
Keynesians: demand for money is elastic (speculative motive). Rate changes have unpredictable effects on spending. Need fiscal backup. Monetarists: demand for money is inelastic. Rate changes reliably affect AD. Fiscal policy is slow and crowds out private investment. Trust monetary rules.
The practical consensus: use both. Monetary policy for normal demand management. Fiscal policy for severe downturns when rates hit zero. Supply-side for long-run growth.
